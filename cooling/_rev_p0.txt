Pool boiling on engineered silicon surfaces: From fundamental mechanisms to
immersion cooling of high-power electronics
--Manuscript Draft--
 
Manuscript Number: RSER-D-26-03910
Article Type: Review Article
Section/Category: Thermal Processes (including biomass, gasification, waste heat recovery)
Keywords: Capillarity;  critical heat flux;  immersion cooling;  pool boiling;  silicon;  surface
engineering
Abstract: Escalating power densities in electronic systems have rendered conventional cooling
technologies increasingly inadequate, motivating renewed interest in two-phase
immersion cooling and other high-heat-flux thermal-management strategies. As silicon
remains the dominant material platform for electronic devices, understanding pool
boiling on engineered silicon surfaces is essential for the thermal design of next-
generation electronic cooling systems. This review examines pool boiling on silicon
substrates, emphasizing surface-engineering strategies spanning nanostructured,
microstructured, hierarchical, and hybrid-wettability designs. By analyzing boiling
behavior through a unified near-field/far-field framework specific to engineered silicon
surfaces, the review distinguishes surface-controlled interfacial mechanisms, including
nucleation, capillary-driven liquid replenishment, and microlayer stability, from larger-
scale vapor-transport constraints that ultimately limit CHF. Water is considered
primarily as a benchmark fluid for elucidating the underlying boiling physics on silicon
surfaces, whereas dielectric fluids are discussed for their direct relevance to
immersion-cooling applications. Cross-scale analysis shows that, for high-surface-
tension fluids, CHF enhancement converges toward a common upper bound,
indicating that surface engineering governs proximity to a hydrodynamic ceiling rather
than redefining the absolute heat-removal limit of the heated component. In contrast,
for low-surface-tension dielectric fluids, CHF decreases systematically with increasing
normalized capillary parameter Bo*, indicating that vapor-removal constraints dominate
the boiling crisis and limit the effectiveness of capillary-based surface strategies.
Finally, future research directions are outlined, emphasizing advanced diagnostics,
cross-scale experimental databases, and reliability-oriented data-driven frameworks for
the predictive design of silicon-based boiling surfaces for high-power electronic
cooling.
